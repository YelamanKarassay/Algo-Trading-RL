1Cover

Q-Learning Paper-Trading Fleet

Today I want to present the Quantphemes RL project as an end-to-end trading research system, not only as a single bot script. The project connects historical research, Q-table training, diagnostics, documentation, and paper-trading deployment through the Quantphemes API.

The key idea is traceability. Every live paper decision should be explainable from the config, the market state, the selected Q-table, and the structured decision log.

2Why This Project Exists

One workflow from hypothesis to paper trading

The project solves three problems at the same time. First, it lets us research many trading hypotheses in a controlled way. Second, it gives us diagnostics so we can understand why a model behaves well or badly. Third, it lets us deploy selected artifacts to paper trading without rewriting the logic by hand.

The most important point is that research and live execution share the same concepts: state encoder, reward function, portfolio rules, agent artifact, and fee model.

3Current Status

Seven-bot paper fleet integrated in one repo

At the moment the repository supports a seven-bot paper fleet. Five strategies are our selected research candidates, and two more are collaborative Q-table models that have been converted into our standard deployment format.

The interval split is important: the five internally selected paper bots are 30-minute models, while the two collaborative imports are 1-hour models. The active assets are 2800.HK, 2828.HK, and 7226.HK. We also researched 7299.HK, but the Quantphemes platform rejected it as a tradeable symbol, so those candidates were halted. That is a useful platform finding, not just a model result.

4Bot Portfolio

Different hypotheses run side by side

The bot portfolio is deliberately not one single strategy. Each bot tests a different hypothesis: broad ETF versus leveraged ETF, 30-minute versus 1-hour cadence, simple state versus volatility-aware state, binary action versus ternary action, and different reward functions.

This helps us observe behavior. If one bot trades too often, or another stays mostly in cash, we can connect that behavior to its reward, state encoder, asset, and action space.

5Bot Interpretation

What each live candidate is meant to teach us

RL_CROSS is the cleanest 30-minute leveraged baseline: 7226.HK, compact three-feature state, binary action, and drawdown penalty. It tells us whether the simplest leveraged setup can find exposure after fees without relying on complex features.

RL_VOL keeps the same 7226.HK setting but adds volatility regime context. This tells us whether the agent behaves more intelligently when it knows whether the market is quiet or active.

RL_FACOV is the most experimental selected bot. It uses 7226.HK, z-score bins, fee-aware reward, and cash/half/full actions. It tests position sizing, but it is also the main fee-watch candidate because it generated the most trades in backtest.

RL_BROAD_A is our 2800.HK broad HSI benchmark, and RL_BROAD_B is the 2828.HK comparison model. These are important because they show how the same framework behaves on less leveraged instruments.

The two collaborative models are 1-hour imported Q-tables. Collaborative HSI 1H gives an external 2800.HK benchmark, while Collaborative Leverage 1H gives an external 7226.HK benchmark. Their provided out-of-sample tests are different in length and date range from our internal 30-minute grid, so I would present them as external reference points, not direct apples-to-apples comparisons.

6Collaborative 1H Test Results

Different windows, useful external benchmarks

For the 2800.HK collaborative 1-hour strategy, the walk-forward out-of-sample period was 2022-05-13 to 2025-06-09, covering 750 trading days. It ended at HKD 952,271, which is a -4.77% net return after HKD 53,801 of fees. It had 491 round-trip trades, a 24.24% win rate, and a profit factor of 0.327. Buy-and-hold net return over that window was +1.69%, so alpha versus buy-and-hold was -6.46 percentage points.

For the 7226.HK collaborative 1-hour strategy, the test period was 2024-04-05 to 2026-02-06, covering 450 trading days. It ended at HKD 968,787, a -3.12% net return after HKD 29,372 of fees. It had 288 round-trip trades, a 15.97% win rate, and a profit factor of 0.164. Buy-and-hold net return over that separate window was +0.44%, so alpha versus buy-and-hold was -3.56 percentage points.

The main message is that these imported policies are technically compatible with our system, but their own out-of-sample tests were net negative after fees. This supports our focus on fee-aware rewards, turnover monitoring, and richer state experiments.

7Leveraged ETF Rationale

Why more paper slots use 7226.HK

The original project focus was 2800.HK because it is the natural broad Hang Seng ETF benchmark. We still keep 2800.HK in the fleet for that reason. But the cost model is demanding: a round trip is about 40 bps, and many ordinary intraday moves in 2800.HK are smaller than that.

Leveraged ETFs such as 7226.HK can move more during the day. That gives the Q-table more room to learn whether exposure is worth the fee and makes state or reward differences easier to observe in paper trading. This does not make leveraged ETFs automatically better or safer; it makes them useful research instruments for testing the model under a stronger intraday signal-to-fee ratio.

8Backtest Snapshot

Selected models beat buy-and-hold in the test window after fees

This slide shows the backtest snapshot for the selected candidates. The numbers are after applying the same fee model used by the environment and live logic. That is important because intraday ETF moves can be small relative to transaction costs.

I would not present these numbers as a final profitability claim. I would present them as a filter for deciding which models are worth observing in paper trading.

9Architecture

Shared core, thin applications

The architecture has a shared core and thin application entry points. YAML configs define what to build. The registry builds the selected data source, state encoder, reward function, and agent. The core trading environment and portfolio accounting are shared by both training and live use.

This avoids one of the biggest risks in trading prototypes: backtest-live drift, where the notebook tests one thing but the production script implements a slightly different rule.

10Data and Configuration

Strategy behavior is declared, validated, and reproducible

Data and configuration are intentionally explicit. For the current research batch, the practical data sources are mainly Webull and Futu exports or downloads. Those files are saved under data/raw and normalized into Hong Kong time before training.

Bloomberg XLSX support exists in the codebase for future terminal exports, but it was not the main source for the current paper-bot fleet. I would describe it as supported infrastructure, not as the data source behind these results.

The YAML files describe the asset, date range, decision times, fee, lot size, state encoder, reward, agent, and artifact paths. This makes experiments easy to review because the hypothesis is visible in the config instead of hidden inside notebook cells.

11YAML Reference

A strategy is visible in one config file

On the YAML slide, I would explain that this is the main reason the project is easy to audit. The config shows the symbol, data file, decision times, fee model, state encoder, reward function, agent type, action count, and artifact locations.

For example, the 2800.HK broad model can be read directly from the YAML: CSV input, 30-minute decision points, 20 bps one-side fee, z-score state encoder, fee-aware reward, Q-table agent, and three actions. That is the complete strategy definition at a high level.

12Plugin Registry

New strategy ideas do not require rewriting the runner

The plugin registry is a small but important design choice. It means the runner can build components by name, such as a CSV data source, a z-score state encoder, a fee-aware reward, or a Q-table agent.

As a result, adding a new experiment usually means adding a plugin and a test, then selecting it in YAML. We do not need to modify the live bot for every new research idea.

13Training Loop

How a Q-table is produced

The training runner loads historical data, groups it into valid trading days, and performs walk-forward training. Each simulated day starts with fresh cash and zero position. At every decision point, the agent chooses an action, the portfolio applies trades and fees, and the Q-table is updated.

Reward timing is carefully defined: portfolio value before the trade is V_t, and portfolio value after the trade is V_t+1. This makes transaction fees appear in the reward at the correct time.

14Live Runtime

How one paper decision is made

In live paper mode, the bot does not train. It loads the Q-table artifact, fetches current price and broker state, builds the market context, encodes that context into a state, reads the Q-table for the current decision point, and chooses the greedy action.

Then it converts that action into a target quantity using cash, price, and lot size. Finally, it patches the Quantphemes holding and writes one JSON log line with the decision details.

15Collaborative Model Integration

External Q-tables now run through the same code path

The collaborative Q-tables were structurally compatible with our agent: five decision points, twenty-seven states, two actions, and visit counts. I converted them into our standard QTableAgent artifact format and created production YAML files for them.

While integrating them, I also fixed a live-state issue in our bot: delta_prev must use the previous decision price, not the current price. This fix benefits both our models and the collaborative imports.

16Framework Choices

Why these tools?

The framework choices are pragmatic. Python, NumPy, and Pandas are strong for market-data workflows. Pydantic and YAML make experiments readable and validated. Pytest and Ruff protect behavior and style. MkDocs Material gives us polished, searchable documentation.

The rationale is not to use tools for their own sake. Each tool reduces a specific project risk: configuration mistakes, data mistakes, trading-rule regressions, or poor maintainability.

17Trading Rules

Financial assumptions are explicit and tested

The financial rules are central to the project. We use a 20 bps one-side fee, and fees are charged only when position changes. The bot respects lot size and available cash. The strategy is day-trading, so live operation targets a flat position near the close.

These rules are tested because small implementation mistakes in fee timing or position sizing can completely change the strategy result.

18Diagnostics

We diagnose the model, not only the final NAV

The diagnostics are important because final NAV alone does not tell us why a model behaved a certain way. We inspect state coverage, oracle upper bounds, feature signal checks, action distributions, reward histograms, and fee pressure.

This helps us separate different failure modes: weak features, sparse training coverage, excessive fees, or a policy that simply learned to avoid exposure.

19Live Operations

Production-style deployment is observable and reversible

The bots run on an Azure VM using systemd services. Each bot has its own config, Q-table artifact, runtime state, and log directory. This means the system can run without keeping a laptop online.

It is also reversible. If a model should be stopped or rolled back, we can stop one service, restore an older artifact, and restart only that bot.

20Quantphemes API Integration

Broker behavior shaped the deployment design

The API integration separates documented broker endpoints from undocumented price endpoints. Holding updates go through a tested sync helper. We also learned that price availability and tradeability are not the same: 7299.HK could be researched, but the platform rejected it for trading.

For the meeting, this is a good point to ask Quantphemes about symbol whitelists, paper execution visibility, and plan-level automation constraints.

21Quality Controls

Trading invariants are protected by tests

The test suite covers the registry, data adapters, portfolio accounting, trading environment, reward timing, Q-table updates, baselines, config loading, report rendering, live-bot logic, runtime state, and federated merge.

This matters because trading bugs are often small but expensive: a fee charged on hold, a value measured after the trade, or a default-to-cash tie rule can quietly distort the whole result.

22Documentation

The project has a public knowledge base

The project documentation is built with MkDocs Material and published through GitHub Pages. It includes architecture, data guide, experiment workflow, live runbook, API notes, research notes, and developer guidance.

This makes the project easier to review and maintain. A future user should be able to understand how to run experiments or operate the bot without reverse-engineering the code.

23Current Boundaries

What the report does and does not claim

The report claims that the research-to-paper pipeline is implemented, tested, documented, and capable of running multiple Q-table strategies. It does not claim that the current strategies are live-profitable.

That distinction is important. The engineering system is ready for serious paper observation, while the trading edge still needs to be evaluated with live paper behavior, fees, and platform constraints.

24Discussion Points for Quantphemes

Platform clarifications that affect paper operation

The main questions for Quantphemes are practical. Which HK ETFs are officially tradeable? How exactly should we verify that a paper PATCH produced a visible order, fill, or holding update? What are the current automation limits? And which endpoints are recommended for intraday price reads?

These answers will help us make the paper deployment more reliable and easier to interpret.

25Closing

We have a working research-to-paper pipeline

The closing message is that the project is ready for a serious technical discussion. We can explain what the models do, how the system is built, how paper decisions are generated, and where Quantphemes platform behavior affects deployment.

I would finish by saying that the system is ready for disciplined paper trading and collaboration, while live strategy edge remains something we will observe carefully.
